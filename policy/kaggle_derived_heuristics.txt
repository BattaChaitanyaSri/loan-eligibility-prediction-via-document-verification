H1. Credit History as a Dominant Risk Signal
Applicants with poor credit history are rejected in the majority of cases, even when income or other factors appear favorable.

Heuristic:
Poor credit history is a dominant negative signal and can independently justify rejection regardless of other positive indicators.

H2. Good Credit History Is Necessary but Not Sufficient
Applicants with good credit history are approved more frequently, but a significant number are still rejected due to other risk factors.

Heuristic:
Good credit history improves eligibility but does not override income instability, repayment burden, or document inconsistencies.

H3. Income Alone Does Not Guarantee Approval
There is substantial overlap in applicant income between approved and rejected cases, including high-income applicants being rejected.

Heuristic:
Higher income reduces perceived risk but does not guarantee approval without supporting credit behavior and repayment capacity.

H4. Loan Amount Must Be Evaluated Relative to Income
Approved and rejected applicants request similar loan amounts in absolute terms; risk emerges when loan amount is disproportionate to income.

Heuristic:
Loan amount must always be evaluated in proportion to verified income rather than as a standalone value.

H5. Repayment Burden Is a Strong Rejection Indicator
Rejected applications show significantly higher loan-to-income ratios, even when other indicators appear acceptable.

Heuristic:
High repayment burden is a strong rejection signal and can outweigh positive credit or income indicators.

H6. Combined Moderate Risks Compound Negatively
Applications with multiple moderate risk indicators are rejected more often than those with a single isolated risk.

Heuristic:
Multiple moderate risks together should be treated as equivalent to a major risk factor.

H7. Employment Type Influences Risk Assessment
Self-employed applicants experience higher rejection rates compared to salaried applicants.

Heuristic:
Self-employed applicants require stronger income verification and are subject to higher scrutiny than salaried applicants.

H8. Education Level Has Limited Decisive Power
Graduate applicants are approved more often, but education alone does not prevent rejection.

Heuristic:
Education level may marginally reduce risk but must not override financial, credit, or document-related concerns.

H9. Property Location Is a Secondary Signal
Approval and rejection occur across all property areas without any location guaranteeing approval.

Heuristic:
Property location should be treated as a supporting signal rather than a decisive eligibility factor.

H10. Credit History Can Override Repayment Capacity
Applicants with poor credit history are rejected even when loan-to-income ratios are relatively low.

Heuristic:
Poor credit behavior can override otherwise acceptable repayment capacity.

H11. Good Credit Does Not Compensate for Excessive Burden
Applicants with good credit history are still rejected when loan-to-income ratios become excessively high.

Heuristic:
Good credit history does not compensate for unsustainable repayment burden.

H12. Absence of Strong Negatives Does Not Guarantee Approval
Some applications are rejected even when no single high-risk factor is extreme.

Heuristic:
Applications that lack strong, corroborated positive evidence across income stability, credit behavior, and repayment capacity should not
be approved by default.

H13. Conservative Decision Bias
Approval occurs only when multiple key indicators align positively.

Heuristic:
In borderline or ambiguous cases, risk must be increased rather than assuming eligibility.
